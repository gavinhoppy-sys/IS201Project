About the Sport

Dirt biking is an off-road motorcycle sport that blends technical skill, physical fitness, and a deep connection with the natural landscape. Riders navigate trails, forests, deserts, and mountains aboard specially designed lightweight motorcycles fitted with knobby tires, long-travel suspension, and minimalist bodywork.

I started riding when I was twelve years old on a borrowed 80cc trail bike in southern Utah. Since then I've upgraded through a 125cc, a 230cc, and now I ride a 300cc two-stroke that absolutely rips through the canyon trails near Moab and the red-rock terrain around Hurricane, Utah. There is nothing quite like the feeling of opening up the throttle on a wide-open desert hardpack — your whole world narrows to the ribbon of trail ahead of you.

Riding has taught me patience, spatial awareness, mechanical problem-solving, and the value of preparation. Every ride is different; every trail demands something new. That mindset — adaptive, curious, methodical — carries directly into how I approach data problems at BYU.

Gear & Pre-Ride Checklist

Proper gear and preparation are the difference between an epic day and an emergency. Here's my complete pre-ride system:

  1. Helmet
    • Full-face motocross helmet (DOT / ECE certified)
    • Check liner for cracks or worn padding
    • Goggles with anti-fog lenses and tear-offs
  2. Protective Gear
    • Chest protector / roost guard
    • Knee and shin guards (knee brace preferred)
    • Elbow pads
    • Neck collar / LEATT brace for technical riding
  3. Riding Apparel
    • Motocross jersey (moisture-wicking)
    • Riding pants with CE hip and knee inserts
    • MX boots (over-the-ankle support)
    • Gloves (grip + palm protection)
  4. Bike Pre-Ride Inspection
    • Check tire pressure (front: 12–14 psi, rear: 12–14 psi for trail)
    • Inspect chain tension and lubrication
    • Verify brake pads and lever travel
    • Check fuel level and coolant (four-stroke)
    • Throttle response and clutch free-play
  5. Trail Pack / Emergency Kit
    • Tube, tire plugs, and CO₂ inflator
    • Basic tool kit (8/10/12mm sockets, Allen keys)
    • First-aid kit and emergency whistle
    • Water (2 L minimum) and snacks
    • Downloaded offline map or GPS device
    • Charged cell phone in waterproof case
Safety first: Always ride with at least one other person on remote trails, and let someone not on the ride know your planned route and expected return time.

Top Trail Tips

After years of riding everything from beginner green loops to gnarly single-track, here are the lessons that have stuck with me:

1. Look Where You Want to Go

The bike follows your eyes. Staring at an obstacle — a rock, a rut, a fallen tree — almost guarantees you'll hit it. Train your eyes 20–30 feet ahead and trust peripheral vision for the immediate terrain.

2. Smooth Is Fast

Jerky throttle inputs, sudden braking, and death-grip handlebar pressure are the enemies of control. Riders who look "slow" on the trail often post the fastest times because smooth transitions keep the suspension working properly.

3. Stand Up on Technical Sections

Standing on the footpegs (slightly bent knees, elbows up) lowers your center of gravity relative to the bike and lets the suspension do its job. Sitting through roots and rocks telegraphs every bump straight to your spine.

4. Know Your Bail-Out Routes

Before committing to an unfamiliar descent or rock garden, hike it first. Identify where you'll dismount if things go sideways. A 30-second scout prevents a 30-minute extraction.

5. Maintain Your Bike Like Your Life Depends on It

Because sometimes it does. A blown fork seal or a snapped brake cable ten miles from the trailhead is at best a long walk and at worst a genuine emergency. The checklist above is not optional.

Bonus tip: Join a local riding club or Facebook group for your area. Trail conditions change seasonally, and local riders always know which sections are washed out, freshly graded, or currently closed.
